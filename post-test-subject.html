When Doctors Use You as a Test Subject

January 28, 2025 · 6 min read

Most Nigerian doctors don't really know what to do with endometriosis. So they just... try things. On you.

That's what it feels like, anyway. Like you're a textbook case they're working through. Except you're not a case. You're a person. And the "trial and error" they're doing? It's your body taking the hit.

The Pattern

Here's how it usually goes: You show up in pain. You explain your symptoms for the hundredth time. They nod, look at your file, and then prescribe something.

Not because they're sure it'll work. Not because they've seen it work for other endometriosis patients. But because it's what they think might help. Or because it's what they were taught to try first.

You take the medication. It doesn't work. Or worse, it makes things worse. So you go back. They try something else. And something else. And something else.

Each time, you're the guinea pig. Each time, your body is the experiment.

The problem isn't that doctors don't care. Most of them do. The problem is that endometriosis isn't taken seriously in medical training. It's not funded. It's not researched enough. So when they encounter it, they're winging it.

The Nigerian Healthcare Reality

In Nigeria, it's even worse. Endometriosis is barely talked about. Most people have never even heard of it. And the doctors who have? They learned about it in passing. Maybe a chapter in a textbook. Maybe not even that.

So when you show up with symptoms, they don't have a playbook. They don't have specialists to refer you to. They don't have access to the latest research or treatment options.

What they have is you. And their best guess.

I've had doctors prescribe medications without explaining the side effects. I've had them push treatments that clearly weren't working because they didn't know what else to try. I've had them dismiss my concerns when I said something wasn't right.

And the worst part? I knew more about my own condition than some of them did. Because I'd spent hours researching online, reading articles, joining forums. While they were treating me like a case study, I was trying to stay alive.

The Unnecessary Surgery

The cystectomy I had in 2022? That's a perfect example. The doctor was confident. "This is what we need to do," they said. No discussion about alternatives. No explanation of risks. Just "this is the plan."

Later, I found out I didn't even need it. But by then, the damage was done. I'd been cut open. I'd spent weeks recovering. I had a scar that would never go away.

All because they were guessing. All because I was the test run.

That's the thing about being treated like an experiment. The doctors move on. They try the same approach on the next patient. Maybe it works, maybe it doesn't. But you? You're stuck with the consequences.

The Mental Toll

Being treated like a test subject messes with your head. You start second-guessing everything. Every recommendation. Every prescription. Every "this should help."

Because if they were wrong before, how do you know they're not wrong now?

You lose trust. Not just in that specific doctor, but in the whole system. And when you're dealing with a chronic condition, when you need ongoing care, that lack of trust is dangerous.

I've skipped appointments because I couldn't face another round of "let's try this and see what happens." I've delayed seeking help because I was tired of being treated like a puzzle instead of a person.

And that's not sustainable. But neither is showing up over and over to be someone's practice patient.

What Needs to Change

Endometriosis needs to be taken seriously. It needs funding. It needs research. It needs to be taught properly in medical schools.

Doctors need to be trained to recognize it, diagnose it, and treat it — not just guess their way through it. And they need to be honest when they don't know. "I'm not sure, but let me refer you to someone who is" should be an acceptable answer.

But more than that, we need to stop treating women's pain like it's less important. Like it's something we can afford to experiment with. Like getting it wrong is no big deal.

It is a big deal. Because it's our bodies. Our lives. Our futures.

What You Can Do

If you're in this situation — if you feel like you're being used as a test subject — speak up. Ask questions. Push back.

"Why this medication and not another?" "What are the side effects?" "Have you treated endometriosis patients before?" "Can I get a second opinion?"

Don't let them rush you. Don't let them make you feel like you're being difficult for wanting answers. You're not a case study. You're a person. And you deserve to be treated like one.

Your body is not a practice field. And you have every right to demand better.

— Mazz
