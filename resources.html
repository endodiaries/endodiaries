Understanding Endometriosis

Let's start with the basics. What is this thing, why does it hurt so much, and why do doctors act like they've never heard of it?

What is Endometriosis? (Explained Like You're Not a Doctor)

The difference between a normal uterus and one with endometriosis

Okay, so normally, the inside of your uterus has a lining called the endometrium. Every month, this lining builds up to prepare for a potential pregnancy. If you don't get pregnant, the lining breaks down and leaves your body ‚Äî that's your period.

With endometriosis, tissue that's similar to that uterine lining starts growing in places it's not supposed to be ‚Äî like on your ovaries, fallopian tubes, the outside of your uterus, your bladder, your bowel, or even further away in rare cases.

Here's the problem: when you get your period, that tissue outside your uterus also bleeds and breaks down, just like the lining inside does. But it has nowhere to go. It's trapped. That causes inflammation, swelling, and scar tissue. And it hurts. A lot.

Over time, this can cause organs to stick together (called adhesions), cysts to form on your ovaries (called endometriomas or "chocolate cysts"), and chronic pain that doesn't just happen during your period.

In simple terms: Your body is growing tissue where it shouldn't, and every month that tissue bleeds internally with nowhere to go. It's painful, exhausting, and often dismissed by doctors who think period pain is "normal."

Common Symptoms

  • Extremely painful periods (not just cramps ‚Äî like can't-move pain)
  • Pain during or after sex
  • Pain when using the bathroom during your period
  • Heavy bleeding or bleeding between periods
  • Fatigue that won't go away
  • Fertility issues
  • Digestive problems (bloating, nausea, diarrhea)

Not everyone has all of these. Some people have severe pain with minimal endo, others have tons of it with little pain. Bodies are weird.

Why Diagnosis Takes Forever

The average time to get diagnosed is 7‚Äì10 years. Why? Because doctors dismiss women's pain, especially Black women's pain. They tell you it's "normal period pain" or "stress" or that you're exaggerating. The only way to officially diagnose endo is through surgery (laparoscopy), and most doctors won't do that until they've tried everything else first.

The Real Problem: Medical Misogyny

Let's be honest ‚Äî endometriosis doesn't get the research funding or attention it deserves because it affects women. If 1 in 10 men had a condition that caused debilitating pain and took a decade to diagnose, there would be billions poured into research. But because it's a "women's issue," it gets ignored.

Doctors aren't trained properly on it. Research is severely underfunded. Treatment options are limited. And women ‚Äî especially Black and African women ‚Äî are told we're overreacting or that pain is just part of being a woman. It's not. This is medical sexism, plain and simple.

Navigating Nigerian Healthcare

Being real ‚Äî most Nigerian doctors don't know much about endometriosis. Here's how to deal with that.

Finding a Doctor Who Listens

Look for gynecologists who specialize in reproductive health or chronic pain. Ask friends, join online endo groups, and don't be afraid to switch doctors if yours isn't taking you seriously.

  • Ask if they've treated endo patients before
  • Bring a symptom diary to appointments
  • Don't downplay your pain

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What are my treatment options?
  • What are the side effects of this medication?
  • Do I really need this surgery?
  • What happens if I don't take this medication?
  • Can you refer me to a specialist?

Red Flags to Watch For

  • "It's just period pain, everyone has it"
  • Prescribing without explaining side effects
  • Rushing you through appointments
  • Dismissing your mental health concerns
  • Pushing surgery or meds you're uncomfortable with

Heat Therapy

Heating pads are my best friend. Hot water bottles, heat patches, warm baths ‚Äî anything that puts heat on your lower abdomen or back can help relax the muscles.

Over-the-Counter Pain Relief

Ibuprofen and paracetamol help, but they're not magic. Take them early, before the pain gets unbearable. Don't wait until you're already dying.

Mental Health & Chronic Illness

Nobody talks about this enough. Living with chronic pain messes with your head.

It's Not Just Physical

Anxiety, depression, feeling isolated, grief over the life you thought you'd have ‚Äî it's all part of this. The medications can make it worse too. You're not overreacting, and you're not weak. This is hard.

Talk to Someone

If you can afford therapy, do it. If not, talk to friends who get it. Join online support groups. Don't keep it all inside.
